The Big Budget Bill:

Here's How $3.5 Trillion Breaks Down

October 7, 2021

by Cailley LaPara

You have probably heard about a convoluted fight in Congress over the budget bill, which goes by numerous names: the reconciliation bill, the budget, the Build Back Better Act, or S. Con. Res. 14, if you’re into that. Perhaps the easiest way to identify it is by its price tag: $3.5 trillion.

The reconciliation bill is not just a bill, School House Rock-style. It is Congress’ annual budget resolution, which cannot be stalled by filibuster in the Senate. Without that obstacle, legislation is easier to pass in a divided Congress. For this reason, majority leaders (Democrats, in this case) throw elements of their agenda into the budget bill, via a process called reconciliation.

To begin this process, Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer (D-NY) sent a memo to Senate Democrats on Aug. 9, allocating amounts from the overall budget to various congressional committees — that’s $3.5 trillion among them. Schumer instructed the committees on how to spend that money. The committees drafted policies based on the amounts allocated. And just like that, the annual budget is numerous progressive policies in one neat, giant package.

The snag is that at $3.5 trillion, the bill will never pass.

On Wednesday, President Biden conceded that the $3.5 trillion would be reduced to somewhere between $1.9 trillion and $2.3 trillion. So, it’s back to the drawing board to see where cuts will be made.

Still, the $3.5 trillion bill was an ambitious plan for sweeping progressive policies. Until we know what will make it into the next draft, it’s worth taking a look at what Biden and congressional Democrats hope to achieve during their time in power. Here is a breakdown of that $3.5 trillion.

$726 B

Committee on Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions

The largest share of the budget goes to the HELP committee for a wide range of social policy, including universal Pre-K, tuition-free community college, investment in school infrastructure, and pandemic preparedness.

$332 B

Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs

The primary focus of this committee’s allocation is affordable housing: creating and preserving buildings by investing in housing programs, and providing financial assistance to improve equity.

$198 B

Committee on Energy and Natural Resources

This committee will allocate funds for various programs in service of moving towards cleaner infrastructure, like energy efficiency and climate research.

$135 B

Committee on Agriculture, Nutrition, and Forestry

This committee will focus on developing policy that conserves agricultural land and increases resiliency against drought and wildfires while reducing carbon emissions.

$107 B

Committee on the Judiciary

Allocations focused on immigration reform, as well as investments in “smart and effective border security.”

$83 B

Committee on Commerce, Science, and Technology

Creating policy to support coastal resiliency and climate research, as well as invest in technology and transportation.

$67 B

Committee on Environment and Public Works

This committee would build policies to provide support for low-income people to adopt “climate-friendly technologies” like solar power. Another focus of this committee is to invest in environmental justice efforts like improving access and affordability to clean water.

$37 B

Committee on Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs

Funds for electrifying federal vehicles, including USPS trucks, improving cybersecurity, and more.

$25 B

Committee on Small Business and Entrepreneurship

Lawmakers hope that this funding can be allocated to create equity in American entrepreneurship. This can be done by providing more capital and direct loans, rather than loans through banks, especially for business owners who did not have relationships with banks during the pandemic and missed out on the Paycheck Protection Program.

$20.5 B

Committee on Indian Affairs

Providing for various social and economic services for Native Americans. Includes allocations for health, education, housing, energy, climate resilience, and language programs.

$18 B

Committee on Veterans Affairs

Funds allocated for upgrades to VA facilities.

-$1 B

Committee on Finance

This committee is responsible for reducing the federal deficit by $1 billion. The most popular way to do this is through tax reform, especially higher taxes for the wealthy and stronger IRS enforcement.

Sources:

Aug. 9 Memorandum to Democratic Senators Re: FY2022 Budget Resolution Agreement Framework
